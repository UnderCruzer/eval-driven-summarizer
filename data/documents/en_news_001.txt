Artificial intelligence will cost jobs, admits Liz Kendall

The UK technology secretary acknowledged that AI deployment will eliminate certain positions, stating: "Some jobs will go." She highlighted concerns about graduate-level roles in law and finance but emphasised that "Others will be created in their place". The government plans to train 10 million workers in basic AI skills by 2030 and aims to make Britain the fastest AI-adopting nation in the G7.

Kendall made the admission during a parliamentary session where she outlined the government's AI strategy, which focuses on retraining workers rather than restricting the technology's adoption. She noted that sectors such as legal research, financial analysis, and administrative roles were already seeing significant disruption from AI-powered tools.

The government's approach has been criticised by trade unions, who argue that the retraining timelines are too slow relative to how quickly AI is replacing workers. Labour MPs have also raised concerns that the 2030 target for training 10 million workers may be insufficient given the pace of technological change.

Kendall defended the policy by pointing to new roles being created in AI development, data analysis, and human-AI collaboration. She also pointed to a £1.2bn investment package announced earlier this year to support workforce transition programmes across the UK.

The admission marks a shift in government rhetoric, which had previously emphasised the economic opportunities of AI without directly acknowledging the displacement risks. Economists have warned that the transition period could see significant structural unemployment, particularly in mid-skill roles that are most susceptible to automation.
